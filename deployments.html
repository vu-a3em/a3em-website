Field Deployments

A3EM audiologgers have been deployed on seven species across three continents — validating hardware and firmware in real conditions, generating annotated acoustic data, and building the collaborations essential to the project's long-term success.

Savanna Elephants Ongoing

Samburu National Reserve, Kenya

The first-ever audiologger deployment on free-ranging elephants, combining GPS tracking with vocalization data to study spatiotemporal variation in wild elephant calls.

4 + 6units deployed across two cohorts
31–36 daysrecorded, first cohort (of 4, one stopped at 7.5 hrs)
PartnerSavannah Tracking Ltd.

A first prototype deployed in 2025 recorded for an estimated 55–58 days before its drop-off mechanism failed to release; notably, on day 55 the collared female gave birth, an acoustically rich event the recording may have captured. A second cohort of 4 units (of 6 built) was deployed in early 2026 in aluminum housings attached inside the GPS collar, with two units co-located with a backup AudioMoth recorder; two have been recovered, one was lost, and one remains deployed.

Forest Elephants Recovery ongoing

Central African Republic

The first-ever application of this technology on forest elephants, following a scheduling delay caused by field-veterinary injuries.

6digital-microphone systems deployed
Jun 30, 2026scheduled drop-off date, all units
1 of 6recovered on first attempt

The remaining five units sit in difficult and sometimes dangerous terrain — one recovery team was charged by elephants during retrieval efforts, which continue.

Bighorn Sheep Multiple rounds

Colorado (captive); Utah & Arizona, Navajo Nation (wild)

Targeting acoustic detection of pneumonia symptoms — coughing — a significant disease concern for bighorn sheep populations in the mountain west.

38 days / 50.81 GBfirst captive ewe, Fort Collins, CO
41 & 29 daystwo captive drop-offs recovered Jul 1, 2026
PartnerColorado Parks & Wildlife

A planned multi-unit round on the Navajo Nation was reduced to a single field deployment by field logistics, with a data-ownership agreement assigning that data to the project. Analysis of prior coughing data also helped surface the cold-temperature analog microphone artifact described on the Research page.

Caribou Complete

Large Animal Research Station, Fairbanks, Alaska

Investigating insect harassment impacts on caribou distribution — insect abundance produces detectable acoustic signatures that complement traditional distribution models.

33 days / 44.68 GBunit 1, 8 kHz sampling
23 days / 59.98 GBunit 2, 16 kHz sampling
< −30°Fextreme cold encountered during deployment

An open-source heating-element drop-off mechanism was tested in this semi-controlled captive deployment; one released cleanly, the other only partially due to cold temperatures and minimal heating-element contact — a design issue since resolved.

Brown Bear New collaboration

Sweden

A no-cost pilot placing acoustic systems alongside an existing 30+ unit GPS collar program, using digital microphones required for the cold environment and partner-supplied drop-offs.

Cross-Cutting Result

Drop-off mechanism reliability

Recovering the device is as critical as recording the data. Tracking release outcomes across deployments led the team to standardize on Telonics for critical elephant work.
